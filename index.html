Components of Working Capital

  • (a) List the main components of working capital (20 marks):
    • Answer: Working capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities.
    • Current Assets: Inventories, trade receivables, cash (e.g., Scholars Ltd.: €176k, €250k, €48k).
    • Current Liabilities: Trade payables, taxation (e.g., Scholars Ltd.: €156k, €14k).
    • Preparation: Memorize components and examples from Part 4 (Scholars Ltd.’s €304k net current assets). Practice listing 3–4 components with definitions. Example: Current assets include cash (€48k for Scholars Ltd.), receivables (€250k), and inventories (€176k).

Role in Sustainability

  • (b) Why is working capital key to sustainability? (20 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Liquidity: Ensures payment of obligations (e.g., Scholars Ltd.’s €156k payables).
      • Operational Continuity: Funds daily operations (e.g., inventory purchases).
      • Growth Support: Enables investment in new opportunities.
      • Risk Mitigation: Buffers against cash shortages (e.g., Marley Ltd.’s low February balance, Part 3).
      • Stakeholder Confidence: Signals financial health to creditors/investors (Part 2, Q3).
    • Preparation: List 4–5 points with examples from Scholars Ltd. or Forrest Ltd. Practice explaining sustainability links. Example: Working capital ensures Scholars Ltd. can pay €156k payables, maintaining supplier trust.

Operating Cash Cycle (OCC)

  • (c) Calculate OCC and suggest improvements for Richmond Retail Ltd. (60 marks):
    • Issue: Financial statement data is missing, but assume similar to Scholars Ltd. (sales €1,825k, cost of sales €1,144k, inventories €176k, receivables €250k, payables €156k).
    • Answer:
      • OCC Formula: Inventory Days + Receivables Days - Payables Days.
      • Calculation:
        • Inventory Days: (€176k ÷ €1,144k) × 365 ≈ 56.15 days.
        • Receivables Days: (€250k ÷ €1,825k) × 365 ≈ 50 days.
        • Payables Days: (€156k ÷ €1,136k) × 365 ≈ 50.12 days.
        • OCC: 56.15 + 50 - 50.12 ≈ 56.03 days.
      • Improvements:
        • Reduce inventory days: Use just-in-time inventory.
        • Shorten receivables days: Tighten credit terms or offer discounts.
        • Extend payables days: Negotiate longer supplier terms.
        • Streamline operations: Automate invoicing or inventory tracking.
    • Preparation: Practice OCC calculations with hypothetical data. List 4–5 improvement strategies. Example: Reducing receivables to 30 days lowers OCC to ~36 days, improving liquidity.

Investor Perspective

  • (a) Explain ratio analysis for investors (50 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Definition: Ratio analysis manipulates financial statement balances to assess performance (profitability, efficiency) and position (liquidity, solvency).
      • Investor Perspective:
        • Profitability: Gross profit margin (Forrest Ltd.’s 32.17%), net profit margin (16.52%), ROCE (32.65%).
        • Liquidity: Current ratio (Forrest Ltd.’s 1.93:1).
        • Efficiency: Receivables (32 days), payables (95 days).
        • Solvency: Debt-to-equity (Forrest Ltd.’s 0.16).
        • Qualitative Factors: Industry trends, management quality.
        • Why Important: Assesses share value, growth potential, and risk.
    • Preparation: Explain 4–5 ratio types with investor relevance, using Forrest Ltd. examples. Discuss qualitative factors. Example: Investors use Forrest Ltd.’s 32.65% ROCE to gauge capital efficiency.

Ratio Calculations

  • (b) Calculate and comment on ratios for Palmer Plastics Ltd. (50 marks):
    • Issue: Financial statement data is missing, but assume similar to Forrest Ltd. (sales €5,750k, gross profit €1,850k, net profit €834k, current assets €2,100k, etc.).
    • Answer:
      • Gross Profit Margin: (€1,850k ÷ €5,750k) × 100 ≈ 32.17%. Moderate profitability.
      • Net Profit Margin: (€834k ÷ €5,750k) × 100 ≈ 16.52%. Good cost control.
      • ROCE: (€950k ÷ €2,910k) × 100 ≈ 32.65%. High return on capital.
      • Current Ratio: €2,100k ÷ €1,090k ≈ 1.93:1. Good liquidity.
      • Acid Test Ratio: (€2,100k - €1,360k) ÷ €1,090k ≈ 0.68:1. Inventory reliance.
      • Inventory Turnover: (€1,360k ÷ €3,900k) × 365 ≈ 127 days. Slow inventory movement.
      • Receivables Days: (€500k ÷ €5,750k) × 365 ≈ 32 days. Efficient collections.
      • Payables Days: (€1,020k ÷ €3,900k) × 365 ≈ 95 days. Long payment terms.
    • Preparation: Practice calculating all 8 ratios from Part 6, with brief commentary. Use tables to present calculations. Example: Palmer’s 0.68:1 acid test suggests liquidity risks if inventory sales slow.

Statement of Cash Flows

  • (a) Importance of Statement of Cash Flows (30 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Tracks Cash Movements: Shows cash inflows/outflows from operating, investing, financing activities (e.g., Marley Ltd.’s €172.4k closing balance).
      • Liquidity Assessment: Ensures ability to pay obligations.
      • Decision-Making: Investors assess cash generation.
      • Solvency: Indicates ability to meet long-term debts.
      • Transparency: Complements profit figures, revealing cash reality.
    • Preparation: List 4–5 reasons with stakeholder examples. Link to Marley Ltd.’s forecast. Example: Investors use Marley Ltd.’s cash flow to assess dividend sustainability.

Daily Cash Monitoring

  • (b) Why monitor cash balance daily? (30 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Avoid Shortages: Ensures funds for wages, suppliers (e.g., Marley Ltd.’s €1.8k February low).
      • Cash Flow Management: Tracks inflows/outflows to prevent overdrafts.
      • Operational Control: Identifies spending patterns or delays in collections.
      • Decision-Making: Informs immediate actions.
      • Risk Mitigation: Prevents insolvency during low cash periods.
    • Preparation: Explain 4–5 reasons, using Marley Ltd.’s cash flow issues as examples. Example: Daily monitoring prevents Marley Ltd.’s February cash dip from disrupting payments.

Quiet Trading Periods

  • (c) Actions for cash in quiet trading periods (20 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Tighten Credit Terms: Reduce receivables days (e.g., Scholars Ltd.’s 50 days to 30).
      • Debt Factoring: Convert receivables to cash.
      • Delay Payments: Extend payables days (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s 95 days).
      • Reduce Inventory: Lower holding costs.
      • Secure Overdraft: Cover temporary shortages.
    • Preparation: List 4–5 actions with practical examples. Example: Factoring Scholars Ltd.’s €250k receivables provides immediate cash.

Cash Surplus Options

  • (d) Options for cash surplus (20 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Invest in Growth: Fund new projects or equipment.
      • Repay Debt: Reduce interest costs (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €400k loan notes).
      • Pay Dividends: Reward shareholders.
      • Build Reserves: Save for future needs.
      • Short-Term Investments: Earn interest on excess cash.
    • Preparation: List 4–5 options, linking to financial management. Example: Marley Ltd.’s €172.4k surplus could fund inventory expansion.

Long-Term Capital Sources

  • (a) Sources of long-term capital for large organizations (20 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Ordinary Shares: Equity from investors (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €1,000k).
      • Preference Shares: Fixed dividend shares.
      • Bank Loans/Bonds: Debt financing (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €400k loan notes).
      • Retained Earnings: Reinvested profits (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €1,510k).
      • Venture Capital: Equity for growth firms.
    • Preparation: List 4–5 sources with examples from Forrest Ltd. Differentiate equity vs. debt. Example: Bonds provide Forrest Ltd. with stable, long-term funding.

Share Capital

  • (b) Pros/cons of issuing share capital (20 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Advantages: No repayment, improves liquidity, reduces debt ratio (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s 0.16).
      • Disadvantages: Dilutes ownership, dividend expectations, issuing costs.
    • Preparation: List 3–4 pros and cons, linking to Forrest Ltd. or Scholars Ltd. Example: Issuing shares boosts Scholars Ltd.’s cash but dilutes control.

Bank Overdraft

  • (c) Bank overdraft explanation and pros/cons (30 marks):
    • Answer:
      • Definition: Short-term facility to overdraw account (e.g., for Marley Ltd.’s cash shortages).
      • Advantages: Flexible, quick, supports working capital.
      • Disadvantages: High interest, repayable on demand, fees.
    • Preparation: Define overdraft and list 3–4 pros/cons with examples. Example: Overdraft helps Marley Ltd. cover €1.8k February shortfall but risks high costs.

Debt Factoring

  • (d) Debt factoring for working capital (30 marks):
    • Answer:
      • How It Helps: Converts receivables to cash, reduces OCC (e.g., Scholars Ltd.’s 50 days), funds payables, supports growth.
      • Drawbacks: Fees reduce profits, affects customer relations.
    • Preparation: Explain 4–5 benefits and 1–2 drawbacks, linking to Part 4. Example: Factoring Forrest Ltd.’s €500k receivables shortens OCC to 52 days.

Marginal Costing and Breakeven

  • (a) Prepare marginal costing P&L and breakeven for Blanch Plant plc (100 marks):
    • Issue: Data is incomplete (selling price €X.00, variable cost €X.30, fixed overheads €3,500,000, general overheads €1,900,000, inventories: 5,000/5,000/7,000 units).
    • Answer (based on Part 5, Q4: €4.5/unit, €2.75/unit, €63,000 fixed, 7,000/60,000/9,000 units, 58,000 sold):
      Item €
      Sales (58,000 × €4.5) 261,000
      Less: Variable Costs (58,000 × €2.75) 159,500
      Contribution 101,500
      Less: Fixed Costs (€40,500 + €22,500) 63,000
      Net Profit 38,500

      Breakeven (Units): €63,000 ÷ (€4.5 - €2.75) ≈ 36,000 units.

      Breakeven (Value): 36,000 × €4.5 = €162,000.

    • Preparation: Practice marginal costing P&L and breakeven with hypothetical data. Show calculations in tables. Example: If Blanch Plant’s selling price is €5, breakeven is €5,400,000 ÷ (€5 - €3) = 2,700,000 units.
