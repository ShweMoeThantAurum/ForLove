Exam Paper and Its Answers

  • Structure: Choose 3 of 5 questions, each worth 100 marks, to be answered in English. The exam tests both theory (e.g., explaining concepts) and numerical skills (e.g., calculations, financial statements).

    • Question 5: Marginal costing and breakeven (numerical, Part 5). - (a) Prepare marginal costing P&L and calculate breakeven for Blanch Plant plc (100 marks, incomplete data).

  • (a) List the main components of working capital (20 marks): - Answer: Working capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities. - Current Assets: Inventories, trade receivables, cash (e.g., Scholars Ltd.: €176k, €250k, €48k, Part 4). - Current Liabilities: Trade payables, taxation (e.g., Scholars Ltd.: €156k, €14k).
    • - Preparation: - Memorize components and examples from Part 4 (Scholars Ltd.’s €304k net current assets). - Practice listing 3–4 components with definitions (e.g., inventories: goods held for sale). - Example: Current assets include cash (€48k for Scholars Ltd.), receivables (€250k), and inventories (€176k).
  • (b) Why is working capital key to sustainability? (20 marks): - Answer (from Part 4, Q2, Q3): - Liquidity: Ensures payment of obligations (e.g., Scholars Ltd.’s €156k payables). - Operational Continuity: Funds daily operations (e.g., inventory purchases). - Growth Support: Enables investment in new opportunities. - Risk Mitigation: Buffers against cash shortages (e.g., Marley Ltd.’s low February balance, Part 3). - Stakeholder Confidence: Signals financial health to creditors/investors (Part 2, Q3).
    • - Preparation: - List 4–5 points with examples from Scholars Ltd. or Forrest Ltd. (Part 6). - Practice explaining sustainability links (e.g., avoiding insolvency). - Example: Working capital ensures Scholars Ltd. can pay €156k payables, maintaining supplier trust.
  • (c) Calculate OCC and suggest improvements for Richmond Retail Ltd. (60 marks): - Issue: Financial statement data is missing, but assume similar to Scholars Ltd. (Part 4: sales €1,825k, cost of sales €1,144k, inventories €176k, receivables €250k, payables €156k). - Answer (based on Part 4, Q1): - OCC Formula: Inventory Days + Receivables Days - Payables Days. - Calculation (using Scholars Ltd. as proxy): - Inventory Days: (€176k ÷ €1,144k) × 365 ≈ 56.15 days. - Receivables Days: (€250k ÷ €1,825k) × 365 ≈ 50 days. - Payables Days: (€156k ÷ €1,136k) × 365 ≈ 50.12 days. - OCC: 56.15 + 50 - 50.12 ≈ 56.03 days. - Improvements: - Reduce inventory days: Use just-in-time inventory (Part 4, Q2). - Shorten receivables days: Tighten credit terms or offer discounts (Part 4, Q1). - Extend payables days: Negotiate longer supplier terms. - Streamline operations: Automate invoicing or inventory tracking.
    • - Preparation: - Practice OCC calculations with hypothetical data (e.g., adjust Scholars Ltd.’s figures). - List 4–5 improvement strategies with practical examples. - Example: Reducing receivables to 30 days lowers OCC to ~36 days, improving liquidity.
  • (a) Explain ratio analysis for investors (50 marks): - Answer (from Part 6, Q1): - Definition: Ratio analysis manipulates financial statement balances to assess performance (profitability, efficiency) and position (liquidity, solvency). - Investor Perspective: - Profitability: Gross profit margin (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s 32.17%), net profit margin (16.52%), ROCE (32.65%) indicate returns. - Liquidity: Current ratio (Forrest Ltd.’s 1.93:1) shows ability to meet obligations. - Efficiency: Receivables (32 days) and payables (95 days) assess cash flow management. - Solvency: Debt-to-equity (Forrest Ltd.’s 0.16) evaluates risk. - Qualitative Factors: Industry trends, management quality (Part 6, Q1a). - Why Important: Helps investors assess share value, growth potential, and risk (Part 2, Q3).
    • - Preparation: - Explain 4–5 ratio types with investor relevance, using Forrest Ltd. examples. - Discuss qualitative factors (e.g., market conditions) to score high marks. - Example: Investors use Forrest Ltd.’s 32.65% ROCE to gauge capital efficiency.
  • (b) Calculate and comment on ratios for Palmer Plastics Ltd. (50 marks): - Issue: Financial statement data is missing, but assume similar to Forrest Ltd. (Part 6: sales €5,750k, gross profit €1,850k, net profit €834k, current assets €2,100k, etc.). - Answer (based on Part 6, Q2): - Gross Profit Margin: (€1,850k ÷ €5,750k) × 100 ≈ 32.17%. Indicates moderate profitability. - Net Profit Margin: (€834k ÷ €5,750k) × 100 ≈ 16.52%. Shows good cost control. - ROCE: (€950k ÷ €2,910k) × 100 ≈ 32.65%. High return on capital. - Current Ratio: €2,100k ÷ €1,090k ≈ 1.93:1. Good liquidity. - Acid Test Ratio: (€2,100k - €1,360k) ÷ €1,090k ≈ 0.68:1. Inventory reliance. - Inventory Turnover: (€1,360k ÷ €3,900k) × 365 ≈ 127 days. Slow inventory movement. - Receivables Days: (€500k ÷ €5,750k) × 365 ≈ 32 days. Efficient collections. - Payables Days: (€1,020k ÷ €3,900k) × 365 ≈ 95 days. Long payment terms.
    • - Preparation: - Practice calculating all 8 ratios from Part 6, with brief commentary (e.g., compare to industry norms). - Use tables to present calculations clearly. - Example: Palmer’s 0.68:1 acid test suggests liquidity risks if inventory sales slow.
  • (a) Importance of Statement of Cash Flows (30 marks): - Answer (from Part 3, Q1): - Tracks Cash Movements: Shows cash inflows/outflows from operating, investing, financing activities (e.g., Marley Ltd.’s €172.4k closing balance). - Liquidity Assessment: Ensures ability to pay obligations (Part 2, Q3, creditors). - Decision-Making: Investors assess cash generation (e.g., operating cash flow). - Solvency: Indicates ability to meet long-term debts (Part 6, debt-to-equity). - Transparency: Complements profit figures, revealing cash reality (e.g., non-cash depreciation, Part 3).
    • - Preparation: - List 4–5 reasons with stakeholder examples (e.g., creditors, investors). - Link to Marley Ltd.’s forecast (Part 3, Q1). - Example: Investors use Marley Ltd.’s cash flow to assess dividend sustainability.
  • (b) Why monitor cash balance daily? (30 marks): - Answer (from Part 3, Q1): - Avoid Shortages: Ensures funds for wages, suppliers (e.g., Marley Ltd.’s €1.8k February low). - Cash Flow Management: Tracks inflows/outflows to prevent overdrafts (Part 7, Q2c). - Operational Control: Identifies spending patterns or delays in collections. - Decision-Making: Informs immediate actions (e.g., delay payments, Part 4). - Risk Mitigation: Prevents insolvency during low cash periods.
    • - Preparation: - Explain 4–5 reasons, using Marley Ltd.’s cash flow issues as examples. - Emphasize internal management focus (Part 5, Q1). - Example: Daily monitoring prevents Marley Ltd.’s February cash dip from disrupting payments.
  • (c) Actions for cash in quiet trading periods (20 marks): - Answer (from Parts 3, 4, 7): - Tighten Credit Terms: Reduce receivables days (e.g., Scholars Ltd.’s 50 days to 30). - Debt Factoring: Convert receivables to cash (Part 7, Q2d). - Delay Payments: Extend payables days (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s 95 days). - Reduce Inventory: Lower holding costs (Part 4, Q2). - Secure Overdraft: Cover temporary shortages (Part 7, Q2c).
    • - Preparation: - List 4–5 actions with practical examples from revision materials. - Link to working capital (Part 4) and financing (Part 7). - Example: Factoring Scholars Ltd.’s €250k receivables provides immediate cash.
  • (d) Options for cash surplus (20 marks): - Answer (from Parts 3, 7): - Invest in Growth: Fund new projects or equipment (Part 4, Q2). - Repay Debt: Reduce interest costs (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €400k loan notes). - Pay Dividends: Reward shareholders (Part 6, investor focus). - Build Reserves: Save for future needs (Part 3, Q1). - Short-Term Investments: Earn interest on excess cash.
    • - Preparation: - List 4–5 options, linking to financial management (Part 3) and stakeholders (Part 2). - Example: Marley Ltd.’s €172.4k surplus could fund inventory expansion.
  • (a) Sources of long-term capital for large organizations (20 marks): - Answer (from Part 7, Q1a): - Ordinary Shares: Equity from investors (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €1,000k). - Preference Shares: Fixed dividend shares. - Bank Loans/Bonds: Debt financing (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €400k loan notes). - Retained Earnings: Reinvested profits (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s €1,510k). - Venture Capital: Equity for growth firms.
    • - Preparation: - List 4–5 sources with examples from Forrest Ltd. - Differentiate equity vs. debt (Part 7, Q1c). - Example: Bonds provide Forrest Ltd. with stable, long-term funding.
  • (b) Pros/cons of issuing share capital (20 marks): - Answer (from Part 7, Q2a): - Advantages: No repayment, improves liquidity, reduces debt ratio (e.g., Forrest Ltd.’s 0.16). - Disadvantages: Dilutes ownership, dividend expectations, issuing costs.
    • - Preparation: - List 3–4 pros and cons, linking to Forrest Ltd. or Scholars Ltd. - Example: Issuing shares boosts Scholars Ltd.’s cash but dilutes control.
  • (c) Bank overdraft explanation and pros/cons (30 marks): - Answer (from Part 7, Q2b): - Definition: Short-term facility to overdraw account (e.g., for Marley Ltd.’s cash shortages). - Advantages: Flexible, quick, supports working capital (Part 4). - Disadvantages: High interest, repayable on demand, fees.
    • - Preparation: - Define overdraft and list 3–4 pros/cons with examples. - Example: Overdraft helps Marley Ltd. cover €1.8k February shortfall but risks high costs.
  • (d) Debt factoring for working capital (30 marks): - Answer (from Part 7, Q2c): - How It Helps: Converts receivables to cash, reduces OCC (e.g., Scholars Ltd.’s 50 days), funds payables, supports growth. - Drawbacks: Fees reduce profits, affects customer relations.
    • - Preparation: - Explain 4–5 benefits and 1–2 drawbacks, linking to Part 4 (OCC, working capital). - Example: Factoring Forrest Ltd.’s €500k receivables shortens OCC to 52 days.
  • (a) Prepare marginal costing P&L and breakeven for Blanch Plant plc (100 marks): - Issue: Data is incomplete (selling price €X.00, variable cost €X.30, fixed overheads €3,500,000, general overheads €1,900,000, inventories: 5,000/5,000/7,000 units). - Answer (based on Part 5, Q4 as proxy: €4.5/unit, €2.75/unit, €63,000 fixed, 7,000/60,000/9,000 units, 58,000 sold): - Marginal Costing P&L:
    Item €
    Sales (58,000 × €4.5) 261,000
    Less: Variable Costs (58,000 × €2.75) 159,500
    Contribution 101,500
    Less: Fixed Costs (€40,500 + €22,500) 63,000
    Net Profit 38,500
    - Breakeven (Units): €63,000 ÷ (€4.5 - €2.75) ≈ 36,000 units. - Breakeven (Value): 36,000 × €4.5 = €162,000.
    • - Preparation: - Practice marginal costing P&L and breakeven with hypothetical data (e.g., assume €5/unit, €3/unit for Blanch Plant). - Show calculations clearly in tables. - Example: If Blanch Plant’s selling price is €5, breakeven is €5,400,000 ÷ (€5 - €3) = 2,700,000 units.
