(a) The Statement of Cash Flows is a critical document as it delineates cash movements across operating, investing, and financing activities, providing transparency on actual liquidity that accrual-based statements may obscure. Users rely on it to evaluate cash generation capacity, solvency, and financial flexibility, informing decisions such as investments, lending, or strategic planning.

(b) From an internal management standpoint, daily cash balance monitoring is imperative to safeguard liquidity, avert overdrafts, and enable prompt responses to variances. It supports accurate budgeting, cost minimization, and operational adjustments.

(c) To secure cash for stakeholders during low-activity periods, implement strategies including expediting receivables collection via discounts, extending supplier terms, curtailing non-essential costs, and establishing contingency reserves.

(d) Management options for cash surpluses encompass investing in interest-bearing instruments, debt repayment, dividend payouts, share repurchases, or allocating funds to capital projects for expansion.

Question Four

(a) Principal sources of long-term capital in a large organization comprise equity shares, retained earnings, long-term debt such as bonds, and hybrid options like convertible instruments.

(b) Issuing additional share capital provides advantages including no fixed repayment or interest burdens and bolstered solvency. Disadvantages involve ownership dilution, heightened dividend pressures, and reliance on favorable market conditions.

(c) A bank overdraft constitutes a flexible short-term credit facility permitting account overdrafts up to a predefined limit. Advantages include immediate liquidity for cash shortfalls with interest solely on utilized funds. Disadvantages include elevated interest charges, risk of facility revocation, and implications for perceived financial stability.

(d) Debt factoring enhances working capital by liquidating receivables for upfront cash, thereby accelerating inflows, alleviating collection burdens, and improving liquidity without incurring further debt.

Question Five

(a) OCR data for Blanch Plant plc is ambiguous (e.g., potential figures like €100 selling price per unit). To prepare a marginal costing profit and loss account: Determine units sold = Opening Inventory + Units Produced - Closing Inventory. Compute sales revenue = Units Sold × Selling Price. Variable costs = Units Sold × Variable Cost per Unit. Contribution = Revenue - Variable Costs. Net Profit = Contribution - Fixed Costs (production + general overheads). Break-even units = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price - Variable Cost per Unit). Break-even value = Break-even Units × Selling Price.

(b) Marginal costing aids in isolating variable costs for pricing and profitability decisions, contrasting with absorption costing by treating fixed costs as period expenses.

(c) Break-even analysis identifies the sales threshold for covering costs, useful for scenario planning, budgeting, and evaluating operational changes.
